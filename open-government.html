The citizens own all the local governments


The citizens, not public officials, are the owners of all the 1,297 municipalities (308 cities, 977 villages, and 12 incorporated towns), 1,431 townships, 102 counties, 856 school districts, 39 community colleges, 358 library districts, 828 fire districts, 340 park districts, 115 forest preserve, conservation, and soil and water districts, 860 drainage districts, 112 sewerage districts, 21 street lighting districts, 20 mosquito control districts, and hundreds of other public bodies in Illinois, 8,511 in all. Most of them have their own elected boards, but seldom have enough candidates at election time. Click here for contact information of those public bodies.


The owners of anything have a right to know what is going on, and to influence decisions being made. Illinois has several "sunshine" laws designed to preserve those owners' rights in their local governments. Using those rights, owners sometimes request information from officials.


The Citizen Participation Institute believes that most public officials want to do the right thing, but they are not always aware of what is required. We work with local governments to educate them and keep them updated so violations don't happen. We work with citizens who need help in those matters.


The Open Meetings Act gives anyone (regardless of where they live) the right to attend meetings of public bodies, to be aware in advance of what will be discussed or voted upon, to address the assembly, and to freely make audio or video recordings of the proceedings,


The Freedom of Information Act generally provides that anyone is entitled to inspect, copy, and have a copy of any public record in the possession or under the control of any public body. That includes emails or texts on an official's private computer or phone that relate to public business. Click here to request a public record.


The Governmental Ethics Act requires all elected officials, candidates, and certain public employees to annually file (usually before May 31 every year), a form that nominally is designed to disclose potential conflicts of interest.


The Citizen Participation Act provides a way to have the court quickly dismiss a suit that was really filed just to scare the defendant from participating in government, petitioning, speaking out, or associating with those who do. The bully can even be ordered to pay the defendant's attorney fees and court costs.
